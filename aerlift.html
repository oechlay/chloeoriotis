August 2020 - Present

Overview

For my fourth-year Capstone project, myself and my team of peers are working with Aerlift Inc., a company that is creating a system that delivers crucial medical supplies from urban hospitals to rural clinics in Rwanda and the Democratic Republic of Congo. We are designing the drone that will be delivering these medical supplies (like blood for transfusions, vaccines, etc.), focusing on aerodynamics and structure while ensuring client needs are met and maximizing efficiency. We are supported by the Centre for Global Engineering within the University of Toronto.

Skills Used

Designing a drone from the ground-up involves a lot of research, especially when no one on the team has done aerodynamics before. The process began with a state-of-the-art analysis, followed by a configuration study for wing placement, size, and fuselage type. Through XFLR (a low-fidelity aerodynamic analysis software) calculations, we narrowed the possible deigns to three plane configurations; conventional, canard, and twin boom. Further optimization in XFLR and solid modelling in SolidWorks showed us the twin boom had the lowest Cd (drag coefficient) and Cm (the moment that causes the plane to pitch) at varying angles of attack, making it the configuration that would become the final design.

We're currently using SolidWorks Flow, a CFD analysis tool, to optimize the drone's components by performing quick, low-fidelity aerodynamics simulations. I'm focusing on the fuselage to minimize the drag forces and keeping mass to a minimum while still leaving room for the payload bay, batteries, and other internal components. Once the team has converged on the optimal SolidWorks models we will move on to ANSYS simulations, which are much higher fidelity and will give us better data to optimize the components. Structural analyses will be conducted to ensure the drone will stay in one piece during its flights and VTOLs (vertical takeoffs and landings).

The ANSYS drone will then be prototyped as a half-scale foam model for glide testing. This data will lead us to a final optimization and design.

What I Learned

Drone design is a highly interdependent design challenge! Designing for one component often requires information from another component, but that information can't be found until the first component is designed. It felt unnatural to hop into this design loop and basically hypothesize information in order to get the process going, since it goes against the typical design process I'm used to, but once the loop is iterated over a few times it becomes clear the information is valid and the process works.

The scope was also so difficult to pin down. Since we began with no knowledge of the huge amount of work this project would bring, the scope had to change constantly as we went through the design process. We decided to focus on depth rather than breadth, since this eight-month project is just too short a timeframe to complete a full design on every aspect of the drone. I'm proud of myself for taking on this project despite having no previous experience with aerodynamics or flight design.
