Overview

I'm really into bioengineering, so for our Kinematics course assignment I convinced my team to model a biological mechanism that's equally fascinating and creepy; the goblin shark's jaw. When biting, the goblin shark extends its upper and lower jaw out and away from its body and quickly snaps it around its prey. I knew this was unique in the animal world so I was excited to dig into the kinematics of how this 'slingshot feeding' worked.

How the Jaw Mechanism Worked

As can be seen in Figure 2, the mechanism of the jaw is driven using a crank rocker consisting of links 2, 3, and 4. Link 2 is the input link, powered by either a motor or a hand-crank with continuous circular motion, and link 4 is the resulting oscillating output. The extended link 3 produces a continuous path at point F that is used to trace the motion of the jaws through added coupling links. Link 5 and link 9 are the output links. They hold the jaws and guide them on the correct path with the assistance of a slider, the dotted center line and a turning pair at point H. The crank rocker motion is mirrored below the centerline, the upper half being the top of the jaw and the bottom part being the lower jaw, by the addition of a second turing pair at point H, as well as the outlined equal ratio gear pair which transfers the velocity and torque to the symmetrical bottom crank rocker.

Analysis Techniques

I used SolidWorks to model the moving mechanism in two-dimensions, ensuring the biological constraints such as jaw angle and extension were accurate. A position analysis, seen in Figure 3, was done in MATLAB to plot the motion of half of the model jaw. Each blue triangle represents the position of point F at equally spaced points in time, given that the input rotation of link two is constant. Each rigid link is represented by a single colour. The density of the triangles provided insight into the time ratio and general relative speed of the mechanism, which was determined to be accurate to the goblin shark's bite speed.
